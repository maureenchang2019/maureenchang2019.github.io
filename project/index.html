Guide to Axolotls

About

The axolotl is a soft-bodied salamander endemic to Lake Xochimilco in Mexico City. Unlike most other salamanders, this amphibian lives its entire life underwater. They have semi-permeable skin which grants oxygen to flow, but their primary mechanisms for breathing are their feathery gills. These gills extend from the axolotl's head and allow for efficient oxygen extraction beneath the surface. An axolotl's diet consists of worms, insects, crustaceans as well as some small fish.

Life Facts

Scientific Name: Ambystoma mexicanum

Size: Up to 12 inches

Weight: Up to 8 pounds

Life Expectancy: 10 - 15 years

Class: Amphibians

IUCN Status: Critically Endangered

Population Decline

Unfortunately, axolotls have been deemed as a critically endangered species according to the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN). The cause of this is due to the ongoing destruction of its habitat. In order to provide for the growing demands in Mexico City, Lake Xochimilco's waters have experienced significant draining and contamination. This pollution has also resulted in a population reduction in other fish native to the lake, therefore restricting the species' food supply.
